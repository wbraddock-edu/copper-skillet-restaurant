Our Story

"Cooking the way Grandma taught us, for the community that raised us."

The Beginning

Bobby Ray's Kitchen

Bobby Ray Harmon was born in Giles County, Virginia, two towns over from Pulaski. He grew up in a small farmhouse kitchen where his grandmother Nell Harmon ran things with cast iron, instinct, and a patience that modern cooks rarely possess. Every morning meant biscuits. Every Sunday meant a roast that had been simmering since Saturday night.

As a boy, Bobby Ray's job was stirring — the grits, the gravy, the pot of black-eyed peas that took all afternoon. But those hours at Nell's elbow weren't just chores. They were an education. He was absorbing generations of knowledge: when the lard is hot enough to fry, how to know biscuit dough by feel, why collard greens need to cook low and slow with just the right amount of smoked ham hock.

At seventeen, Bobby Ray left Pulaski to study at New River Community College and later took a job as a prep cook at a steakhouse in Roanoke. He worked his way up to line cook, then sous chef, learning French techniques and modern plating while never forgetting the food that had shaped him. "You can learn to make a beurre blanc," he says, "but nothing teaches you like watching your grandmother make gravy with her eyes closed."

Opening Day, 2009

Coming Home to Cook

In 2008, Bobby Ray's grandmother Nell passed away, leaving him her wooden recipe box — three generations of handwritten index cards, some stained with decades of kitchen use. For Bobby Ray, it was both an inheritance and a mandate. "She kept saying, 'Don't let these die with me,'" he recalls. "So I figured the best way to honor her was to feed people with them."

Bobby Ray returned to Pulaski in early 2009 with his savings, Nell's recipe box, and a lease on a vacant corner space at 75 Washington Avenue that had once been a hardware store. He stripped it down to the bones, built a bar-height counter along one wall, installed the commercial kitchen himself with help from his cousin Dale, and hung a copper skillet on the wall that Nell had given him years before.

The name was never in question. "That skillet is the whole thing," Bobby Ray says. "It's what Southern cooking is — heavy, sturdy, does the work quietly, and makes everything taste better."

The Copper Skillet opened on March 14, 2009, a Thursday morning. By 10 AM, they'd run out of biscuits. By noon, they'd run out of chicken fried steak. Bobby Ray called his cousin for help and worked sixteen hours straight. "Best day of my life," he says. "Still is."

What We Stand For

Our Philosophy

From Scratch. Always.

We don't open cans or use mixes. Every sauce, every gravy, every pie crust starts from scratch in our kitchen. It takes longer. It's worth it. Our customers can taste the difference, and that's the only standard we care about.

Local First

We source ingredients from farms within 100 miles of Pulaski whenever possible. That means produce from Giles County, beef from Floyd County, and peaches from the Shenandoah Valley. Keeping money local keeps our community strong.

Hospitality Without Pretense

We are not a fancy restaurant. We are a neighborhood restaurant with high standards. Everyone who walks through our door deserves to feel at home. We don't rush tables, we don't upsell, and we always refill your sweet tea without being asked.

Community Is Family

Pulaski made The Copper Skillet possible. We give back through Little League sponsorships, donations to the Pulaski Food Pantry, and school fundraiser nights where 15% of sales go directly to local classrooms.

Giving Back

In the Community

Pulaski Little League Sponsor — We've sponsored the Harmon's Skillet team in the Pulaski Little League for seven consecutive seasons. Bobby Ray coaches third base on Wednesday evenings.

Pulaski Food Pantry — Every Friday evening, we donate unsold prepared food to the Pulaski County Food Pantry. Since 2015, we've contributed over 40,000 meals to families in need.

School Fundraiser Nights — Once a month, a local school partners with us for a fundraiser night. We donate 15% of all sales that evening directly to the school's activity fund.

Annual Harvest Dinner — Every November, we host a free Thanksgiving dinner for Pulaski seniors. Open seating, no reservation needed. "Everyone eats well on Thanksgiving." — Bobby Ray
